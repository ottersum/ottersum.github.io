CHAPTER 2.5

Positive feedback loop in imposter syndrome

2025/03/24

While researching imposter syndrome to write this ramble, I was slightly perplexed to see the definition below.

Imposter syndrome (IS) is a behavioral health phenomenon described as self-doubt of intellect, skills, or accomplishments among high-achieving individuals. These individuals cannot internalize their success and subsequently experience pervasive feelings of self-doubt, anxiety, depression, and/or apprehension of being exposed as a fraud in their work, despite verifiable and objective evidence of their successfulness. (Hueker et al.)

I used to think imposter syndrome was something that anyone can experience regardless of whether or not they were actual imposters. But this definition implies that a person experiencing imposter syndrome is “high achieving,” making imposter syndrome somewhat of a fallacy. This version makes more sense given that my positive feedback loop is founded upon the conditional that one is not an imposter.

My experience?

I do experience imposter syndrome, but I cannot say that I experience it every day>. I have been fortunate enough to receive decent grades, genuinely enjoy certain academic things, and earn recognition or be accepted to a non-zero number of things I attempt to do. 1 By default2, I feel exactly like the big fish in a small pond who belongs in the higher percentile (often) only within its insular community.

However, this, I feel, makes me more prone to imposter syndrome.

First, because I often am characterized as “getting good grades” or being “good at studying” within my community, I am less open to sharing my achievements3. Although there are other reasons4, not sharing things about myself mostly pertains to my fear of perpetuating this high standard that others hold against me and the proportionally higher disappointment that comes from future failure.

This applies to the opposite situation as well. Once I exit my small pond, I very quickly become (or seem to become) the imposter. The unconscious and unspoken pride I might have had diminishes when I realize that there are much much larger fish in the sea.

During camp, I experienced the latter, which led to many 1-on-1s and dinner conversations about combating imposter syndrome. And from one of the 1-on-1s came the memorable idea of “own the trickster image”, where I acknowledge the fact that I am an imposter5. Interestingly, embracing the “imposter” in me by actively trying to block out the fear of being exposed taught me some important stuff.

feedback loop within the small pond?

My positive feedback loop went like this:

  1. There is action B: I avoid talking about myself 6 which is caused by either of the two below.

    1. I believe in the notion A that I am an imposter, so I perform B

    2. In attempting to be humble / emotionally guarded etc, action B is performed in non-imposter syndrome situations too.

  2. Regardless of cause or situation, Action B reinforces and exacerbates belief A in two ways.

    1. Affirming the consequent: Since I perform B because of A, the fact that I perform B wrongly affirms the consequent A that I am an imposter.

    2. Self-fulfilling prophecy: The fact that I avoid talking about myself obscures myself from myself. this creates the impression that I am truly insignificant, thus self-fulfilling prediction A.

In written form, the flaws in my thinking are made clear7. Action B is caused by many different ideas; B is incorrectly and only attributed to A at the end.

Thankfully in camp, the counterintuitive action of embracing A decreased A. This seems to be because, in acknowledging that I am not A, I did not perform B. Not performing B provided me with a more accurate version of myself, which in turn revealed new and interesting parts that did not always warrant imposter syndrome. What a discovery.

However at this point, alleviating imposter syndrome has only been possible when I leave my small pond. When I’m still in my small pond, I am more prone to compare myself to others, feel a sense of atychiphobia (fear of failure) or perfectionism. I assume that this is largely because “accepting that I am an imposter” seems less plausible when I belong in the "smaller fish" pool. Because I cannot fully accept that I am an imposter, I still engage in the feedback loop that results in the contradictory belief that I am an imposter. Still working on solving this issue.

Final comments

As I was transcribing my thoughts, I noticed my mind kept floating to three more tangents.

  1. The connection between imposter syndrome, maladaptive perfectionism and more importantly, the need for cognitive closure (something I had been planning to write about). These seem to have some connections that I cannot quite articulate yet in length.

  2. Emotional guardedness and fear of judgment as something I struggle with daily.

  3. Being scared of things I haven’t experienced (mainly larger-scale failure with bigger implications)

Maybe more thoughts on this soon…
